Cliff Flow CFD & Recirculation Analysis

This project presents a 2D CFD study of wind flow over a cliff, using a simplified step geometry to replicate key flow features such as separation and recirculation zones.

The analysis supports site evaluation for future wind turbine installation by identifying regions of flow acceleration. Based on prior studies and simplified atmospheric boundary conditions, the conceptual model was defined with key flow phenomena: upstream separation, wake development, and stagnation zones at the cliff face.

A structured mesh was generated with refinement near solid walls to ensure accurate resolution of boundary layer effects and correct y‚Å∫ values for turbulence modeling. Several mesh configurations were tested to balance computational cost and accuracy.

Special attention was given to mesh quality around the step, where gradients are strongest, and convergence behavior was monitored through residual analysis during simulation.

Velocity magnitude contours reveal a significant increase in wind speed immediately above the cliff, identifying this area as a potential hotspot for wind energy capture. The simulation confirms the recirculation bubble forming behind the step, typical of bluff body flows.

This stage solidified my CFD post-processing skills, particularly in identifying regions of interest for engineering applications through contour plots and vector fields.

The final analysis maps the turbulent intensity across the domain, highlighting how recirculation affects flow stability. Higher turbulence levels were found in the wake region, indicating increased mechanical stress that would affect turbine performance and maintenance cycles.

This experience enhanced my understanding of turbulence modeling, mesh sensitivity, and how physical simplifications impact engineering decisions in wind energy siting.
